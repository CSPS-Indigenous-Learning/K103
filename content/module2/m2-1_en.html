Contact and partnership

For the next two hundred years, the Hudson’s Bay Company, whalers and explorers were the main European contacts for some Inuit such as the Kivallirmiut or Caribou Inuit in Nunavut. Christian missionaries also arrived to spread their religion, often pushing aside traditional beliefs and practices.

Trading with the Europeans provided the Kivallirmiut with firearms and allowed them to hunt caribou more efficiently. Through the Hudson’s Bay Company, they had access to manufactured goods, foodstuffs and tools that helped them in their day-to-day lives.

However, by the early 20th century, the population of Kivallirmiut was nearly decimated. The reasons included the following:

  • Many Inuit left and travelled south to follow the caribou and trade furs with the Europeans
  • Inuit came into contact with European diseases
  • Caribou became scarce due to overhunting and the disruption of their habitat by Europeans

From 1915 to about 1924, a severe famine killed nearly two thirds of the population. At this point, Inuit became increasingly dependent on European trade.
