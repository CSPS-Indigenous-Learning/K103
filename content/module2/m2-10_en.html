Inuit and Residential Schools

Starting in 1951, Inuit children were sent to residential schools like First Nations children. As Inuit moved into permanent settlements, the childrenâ€™s attendance at residential schools became mandatory.

In 1964, 75% of Inuit children attended a residential school. Residential schools systematically undermined Inuit culture across Canada and disrupted families for generations. They severed the ties through which Inuit culture is taught and sustained and contributed to a general loss of language and culture.

Many of the survival skills necessary for life in the North were passed down from adult to child through hands-on experience. With the children away from their homes and family, these skills were not transferred to the next generation. When the children returned home from the schools, they found themselves lacking necessary skills. This accelerated the move away from traditional lifestyles and increased dependence on government support.
