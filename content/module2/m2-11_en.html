Question of

In 1941, Inuit were assigned a number by the government called Eskimo disk numbers. They were instructed to wear this at all times and were expected to use their number when dealing with the government instead of their name.

 

Answer: True.

Eskimo disk numbers were intended to replace the Inuit naming traditions.

One of the most profound changes to Inuit culture was the government-led initiative that created permanent residency for Inuit communities and villages.

Answer: True.

In the 1950s, the Government of Canada revised policies that would see Inuit settled in one location.

Inuit hunters were used to travelling freely through the terrain by dogsled and kayak to go where the food could be found. They would change their camp locations throughout the year to give better access to food supplies. The Government of Canada was not accustomed to this lifestyle and did not intend to support it.

In 1964, 75% of Inuit children attended a residential school.

 

Answer: True.

Many of the survival skills necessary for life in the North were passed down from adult to child through hands-on experience. With the children away from their homes and family, these skills were not transferred to the next generation.
