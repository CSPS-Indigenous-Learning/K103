Getting to know Inuit culture

Inuit have always been innovative and resourceful in facing the challenges of their environment. For example, Inuit created the kayak, a single-person watercraft made of scavenged driftwood, whale bone and marine mammal skins. Traditionally, kayaks allowed Inuit hunters to move freely in the difficult waters of the Arctic. Today, they are used throughout the world for recreation and sports competitions.

Here are a few iconic Inuit cultural symbols and practices. There are many more to discover!

Inuit societal values

Since its creation in 1999, the Government of Nunavut has been guided by eight Inuit societal values. These values incorporate traditional knowledge and practices, providing guidance on how people should live and work with each other and the environment.

These values also serve to promote Inuit culture and strengthen the role of Elders.

Qulliq

The qulliq is a traditional seal-oil lamp made of stone. It was used by Inuit women as a source of light, fuel to cook with and heat for their homes.

Now, the qulliq is mainly used for ceremonial purposes.

Throat singing

Throat singing, also called katajjaq, is a face-to-face competition between two women or girls using the breath, voice and throat.

Inuksuk

The inuksuk is a symbol of Inuit identity.

The inuksuk is made up of large rocks piled one on top of the other. It was traditionally used to help travellers find their way to a source of water, a caribou passage or a place to camp.
